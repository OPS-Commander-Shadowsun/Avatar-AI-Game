# Fire Nation Military

## Nature

The Fire Nation Military is the land, naval, airship, logistics, engineering, and fortress apparatus built during the Hundred Year War. Six years after the war it is undergoing demobilization, doctrinal reform, war-crimes investigations, and conversion of military industry toward civilian use.

## Composition

- army infantry and firebending units;
- navy crews, marines, shipyards, and coastal commands;
- airship crews and technical personnel;
- engineers, armorers, medics, quartermasters, messengers, and transport officers;
- academies, garrisons, prison personnel, and intelligence offices;
- veterans, reserves, and demobilized units.

## Activities

Defend Fire Nation territory, protect shipping, dismantle illegal occupation networks, secure dangerous weapons, support disaster response, patrol borders, escort officials, and retrain soldiers for peacetime service.

## Structure

The Fire Lord is commander by office, but daily control passes through ministers, generals, admirals, regional commands, captains, and junior officers. Rival chains of loyalty may remain from Ozai's reign.

## Joining

Citizens may enlist through recruiting offices or military academies. Specialist entry exists for engineers, healers, navigators, and experienced foreign advisers under exceptional agreements. Requirements include age, fitness, identity review, training, oath, and assignment. Firebending can affect role but is not required.

## Ranks and advancement

Recruit, soldier or sailor, specialist, noncommissioned officer, junior officer, captain or commander, senior command, general or admiral. Advancement depends on training, service, examinations, performance, sponsorship, and politics.

## Character affiliations

- **Zuko:** commander-in-chief by office and principal reform authority.
- **Ozai:** former supreme commander; loyalist networks may still invoke him illegally.
- **Azula:** former royal commander with extensive operational knowledge but no lawful command by default.
- **Iroh:** retired general; no active command, though veterans may revere him.
- **Jeong Jeong:** former admiral and deserter; critic of destructive doctrine.
- **Mai:** possible palace-security ally, not automatically military.
- **Aang, Katara, Sokka, Toph, and Suki:** foreign allies or oversight partners without ordinary command authority.

## Internal blocs

Professional reformers, exhausted veterans, imperial loyalists, career officers protecting status, young recruits without wartime loyalty, industrial interests, and units worried that demobilization will destroy their livelihoods.

## Discipline and leaving

Members are subject to military law. Desertion, abuse of civilians, loyalist conspiracy, trafficking weapons, and refusal of lawful demobilization can trigger court-martial. Honorable discharge, retirement, medical separation, or transfer to civil defense are normal exit paths.

## Player interaction

The player may enlist, accept a specialist commission, work as an external investigator, or oppose a rogue unit. Rank grants authority only within assignment and chain of command.

## AI rules

- The military is powerful but not politically unified.
- Orders require logistics and communications.
- Soldiers have families, pay concerns, trauma, loyalties, and personal judgment.
- Reform cannot instantly erase imperial doctrine.
- Military membership creates legal duties and limits personal freedom.
