# Southern Water Tribe

## Overview

The Southern Water Tribe is a growing polar settlement rebuilding after decades of raids, population loss, disrupted bending traditions, and wartime absence. Six years after the war, returning warriors, healers, traders, builders, Northern advisers, refugees, and young families have transformed the once-small village into a larger but still fragile community.

## Geography and climate

The settlement stands amid sea ice, snow plains, coastal waters, hunting grounds, and shifting seasonal routes. Blizzards, thin ice, extreme cold, limited daylight, and sudden storms can isolate neighborhoods or delay ships. Safe travel depends on local knowledge.

## Architecture

Homes and public structures use ice, snow, timber, hide, bone, stone, and imported materials. New construction may include larger communal halls, clinics, training spaces, docks, workshops, warehouses, and defensive barriers. Rapid growth creates uneven quality and disputes over land and labor.

## Population and culture

The population includes elders, children, returning warriors, widows, fishers, hunters, healers, artisans, waterbending students, traders, and families reunited after the war. Culture emphasizes communal survival, kinship, storytelling, practical skill, and shared food, but generations disagree over outside influence and modernization.

## Government

Hakoda serves as chief or leading political authority by default, supported by elders, war veterans, household leaders, healers, hunters, and emerging civic councils. Gran Gran holds significant moral and cultural influence without requiring formal office.

## Economy

Fishing, hunting, preserved food, furs, bone craft, boat repair, healing, local construction, and trade support the settlement. Imports may include metal tools, medicines, timber, cloth, grain, and technical equipment. Shortages can rapidly increase prices or force rationing.

## Security

Local warriors, watch groups, experienced hunters, waterbenders, and visiting allies protect the tribe. Threats include smugglers, exploitative merchants, unresolved wartime enemies, dangerous weather, sea predators, and political agents seeking influence.

## Daily life

Days begin with weather checks, fishing, hunting, fuel preparation, child care, repairs, lessons, and supply allocation. Evening meals, storytelling, planning, and community disputes often occur in shared spaces. Severe weather can suspend normal routines.

## Travel connections

Travel is possible by sled, small boat, larger vessel, animal transport, or rare allied air travel. Routes connect to hunting grounds, remote camps, coastal waters, and international shipping. Weather and sea ice determine actual access.
