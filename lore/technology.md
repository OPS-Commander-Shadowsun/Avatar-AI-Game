# Technology and Industry

## Purpose

This chapter defines the technological level of the world six years after the Hundred Year War. It supports travel, industry, warfare, medicine, communication, infrastructure, crime, reconstruction, and invention without importing later-era technology.

Technology should develop from existing materials, bending traditions, engineering knowledge, labor, and political need. Progress is uneven. A machine existing in one military project does not mean it is cheap, common, or available everywhere.

## Core technological level

The setting combines:

- handcraft;
- animal-powered transport;
- bending-assisted construction;
- advanced metalworking;
- steam power;
- mechanical engines;
- explosives;
- armored vehicles;
- naval industry;
- specialized airships;
- large civil works.

It does not include modern electronics, digital systems, radio networks, automobiles in the later modern sense, aircraft industries, modern firearms, mass electrical grids, or later-era urban infrastructure.

## General technology rules

The AI must consider:

- who designed the device;
- what materials it requires;
- who can manufacture and repair it;
- how it is powered;
- how heavy it is;
- how it is transported;
- whether it was military, civilian, experimental, or common;
- who controls the knowledge;
- what maintenance it needs;
- what environmental or political consequences it creates.

A prototype is not automatically mass-produced.

## Fire Nation engineering

The Fire Nation possesses the most developed industrial and military engineering base.

Established strengths include:

- steamships;
- metal hulls;
- boilers and engines;
- armored vehicles;
- large drills;
- factories;
- cranes and mechanized loading;
- explosives;
- advanced metallurgy;
- military logistics;
- airships developed from stolen or adapted designs;
- organized shipyards and arsenals.

This capacity was built for imperial war. Six years later, conversion to peaceful use is a central political issue.

### Industrial conversion

Former war industries may convert to:

- cargo ships;
- agricultural machinery;
- water pumps;
- heating systems;
- construction cranes;
- mining equipment;
- public transport experiments;
- medical instruments;
- firefighting tools;
- disaster-response equipment.

Conversion is expensive and contested. Factory owners may resist. Workers may fear unemployment. Former officers may divert weapons. Reformers may demand inspections and worker protections.

## Earth Kingdom engineering

Earth Kingdom technology varies enormously by region.

Strengths include:

- stone construction;
- walls and fortifications;
- roads and bridges;
- canals and irrigation;
- mining;
- quarrying;
- carts and animal transport;
- bending-assisted architecture;
- local mechanical systems;
- Omashu's delivery network;
- large urban works in Ba Sing Se.

Earthbending can replace some machinery, but access to skilled benders is uneven. Large projects still require planning, labor, materials, surveying, and maintenance.

### Bending-assisted construction

Earthbenders can:

- shape foundations;
- move stone;
- create temporary barriers;
- clear roads;
- dig channels;
- stabilize slopes;
- support mining.

They cannot automatically:

- create refined metal;
- guarantee structural safety;
- replace carpentry, engineering, drainage, or skilled design;
- work indefinitely without rest.

## Water Tribe technology

Water Tribe technologies are adapted to ocean travel, ice, severe cold, hunting, fishing, preservation, and compact community life.

Common strengths include:

- canoes and ships;
- sails and rigging;
- insulated clothing;
- hide and bone tools;
- harpoons;
- fishing gear;
- food preservation;
- ice architecture;
- navigation by stars, currents, animals, and weather;
- practical medical tools;
- waterbending-assisted construction and transport.

Northern infrastructure is more formal and monumental. Southern technology is often portable, repairable, and adapted to scarcity.

### Waterbending and technology

Waterbenders may assist with:

- moving vessels;
- controlling ice;
- firefighting;
- irrigation;
- cleaning;
- cooling;
- healing;
- rescue.

Their presence does not eliminate the need for boats, tools, storage, or skilled nonbenders.

## Air Nomad technology

Air Nomad technology favored lightness, mobility, simplicity, architecture, and cooperation with animals.

Known examples and principles include:

- gliders;
- staffs;
- light clothing;
- temple mechanisms;
- elevated architecture;
- air-current knowledge;
- sky bison saddles and travel gear;
- compact personal possessions;
- devices designed around airbending.

Many techniques and maintenance traditions were lost with the Air Nomads. Aang may recognize objects without knowing every manufacturing detail.

Air Nomad artifacts should not be treated as treasure without cultural consequences.

## Transportation

### Walking and animal transport

Most people travel by foot, cart, riding animal, or small boat.

Common limits include:

- terrain;
- weather;
- food;
- animal health;
- road quality;
- banditry;
- border controls.

### Ships

Ships range from small fishing vessels to large Fire Nation steamships.

Steamships require:

- fuel;
- engineers;
- replacement parts;
- ports capable of servicing them;
- trained crews.

Sailing vessels remain common because they are cheaper and easier to maintain.

### Armored vehicles

Fire Nation armored vehicles are military machines designed for difficult terrain and assault.

After the war, surviving vehicles may be:

- dismantled;
- stored;
- converted;
- sold illegally;
- used by loyalists or criminals;
- studied by foreign engineers.

They should remain rare outside military or industrial contexts.

### Airships

Airships were late-war weapons enabled by stolen or adapted designs and Fire Nation industry.

At campaign start they are:

- rare;
- expensive;
- politically sensitive;
- difficult to maintain;
- vulnerable to weather and sabotage;
- associated with Ozai's final campaign.

Civilian airship travel is not yet ordinary.

### Flying bison

Appa is not public transportation. Flying bison are culturally sacred and nearly absent. Aang decides who may travel with Appa.

## Communication

Communication relies on:

- messengers;
- couriers;
- ships;
- riders;
- messenger hawks and other trained animals;
- signal fires;
- flags;
- drums;
- horns;
- written orders;
- official relay systems.

Communication has delays. Messages can be lost, forged, intercepted, mistranslated, or outdated.

There are no radio networks or instant long-distance communication systems.

## Printing, records, and literacy

Books and documents may be handwritten, block-printed, copied by specialists, or produced through regional printing methods.

Literacy varies by class, region, gender, occupation, and access to schooling.

Important records include:

- land deeds;
- military orders;
- tax records;
- shipping manifests;
- family registers;
- court judgments;
- maps;
- school texts;
- bending manuals.

War damage and censorship have destroyed or falsified many records, creating legal and historical disputes.

## Medicine

Medicine includes:

- herbal remedies;
- surgery and wound care;
- splints;
- sanitation;
- midwifery;
- healing waterbending;
- massage and chi knowledge;
- traditional regional practices.

Healing waterbending can accelerate recovery and diagnose some problems, but it does not make all medicine obsolete.

Limits include:

- exhaustion;
- need for clean water;
- severity of injury;
- infection;
- poison;
- chronic illness;
- lack of trained healers;
- permanent scars or disability.

Pregnancy, childbirth, and infant care require time, knowledge, support, and risk management.

## Weapons

Common weapons include:

- swords;
- spears;
- staffs;
- clubs;
- bows;
- crossbows where appropriate;
- knives;
- boomerangs;
- shields;
- military explosives;
- siege equipment.

Fire Nation explosives and mechanized weapons are controlled but circulate through black markets.

Do not introduce modern firearms as common technology.

## Armor and protective equipment

Armor varies by nation, rank, climate, and role.

It may include:

- metal plate;
- scale armor;
- leather;
- layered fabric;
- helmets;
- shields;
- cold-weather protection;
- specialized naval gear.

Armor reduces some injuries but limits movement and does not make a person immune to bending.

## Metalbending and industry

Toph's discovery creates major technological possibilities, but metalbending remains specialized.

Potential uses include:

- repair;
- rescue;
- construction;
- precision manufacturing;
- security;
- mining;
- sabotage;
- law enforcement;
- warfare.

Limits:

- training is rare;
- not all metal contains equally detectable impurities;
- fine control requires exceptional perception;
- metalbenders remain vulnerable to terrain, fatigue, and nonmetal materials;
- institutions based on metalbending are not predetermined.

## Firebending and industry

Firebenders can provide heat for:

- forging;
- cooking;
- boilers;
- ceramics;
- glasswork;
- emergency heating.

This creates skilled industrial occupations but also safety risks and labor disputes. Firebenders are not infinite fuel sources.

## Environmental impact

Industry can cause:

- air pollution;
- contaminated rivers;
- deforestation;
- mining collapse;
- habitat loss;
- spirit disturbances;
- worker illness;
- farmland damage;
- dangerous waste.

Environmental damage should create economic, political, medical, and spiritual consequences.

## Technology transfer

After the war, engineers, tools, and captured machines cross borders.

Technology transfer may occur through:

- legal trade;
- diplomatic exchange;
- former prisoners;
- defectors;
- industrial espionage;
- salvaged wreckage;
- black markets;
- joint reconstruction projects.

Foreign adoption is not immediate. Local materials, skills, infrastructure, and culture shape how inventions are used.

## Inventors and prototypes

Inventors may create plausible new devices by combining established principles.

A prototype should require:

1. a clear purpose;
2. available materials;
3. relevant expertise;
4. time and funding;
5. testing;
6. failures and revisions;
7. maintenance planning.

Player inventions may succeed, but not through vague declarations.

## Technological secrecy

Military and industrial knowledge may be protected by:

- classified archives;
- guarded factories;
- guild rules;
- family techniques;
- destroyed plans;
- political censorship;
- personal loyalty.

Obtaining designs may create espionage, theft, diplomacy, or moral choices.

## Common postwar technology conflicts

- converting weapons factories;
- disposing of explosives;
- repairing occupied infrastructure;
- ownership of captured ships;
- foreign engineers accused of espionage;
- workers harmed by unsafe machinery;
- stolen airship components;
- metalbending used for surveillance or imprisonment;
- pollution angering local spirits;
- nobles monopolizing new tools;
- criminals acquiring military surplus;
- communities rejecting machines associated with occupation.

## Technology progression rules

The AI must:

- build new inventions from existing principles;
- keep prototypes rare until production capacity exists;
- track fuel, materials, labor, and maintenance;
- avoid instant technological revolutions;
- avoid importing later-era devices;
- create social consequences for disruptive inventions;
- distinguish military availability from civilian availability;
- respect regional inequality in infrastructure.

## Prohibited default technologies

Do not introduce as normal background technology:

- radio;
- telephones;
- modern electrical grids;
- electric lighting networks;
- automobiles resembling later-era cars;
- airplanes;
- modern firearms;
- photography or cinema unless explicitly justified by a campaign-specific invention;
- computers;
- modern medical machinery;
- mass-produced later-era urban infrastructure.

Any exceptional invention approaching these categories requires a major, carefully justified campaign arc and must not become retroactively common.
