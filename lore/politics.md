# Postwar Politics

## Core Principle

The end of the Hundred Year War did not create a global government or a universally accepted peace settlement. Each nation retains its own rulers, customs, laws, military structures, factions, and internal conflicts. Political legitimacy is local, contested, and shaped by wartime experience.

The AI must never reduce world politics to a simple alliance of good nations against evil Fire Nation remnants. Every society contains victims, opportunists, reformers, traditionalists, idealists, criminals, exhausted civilians, and people whose interests conflict even when their intentions are understandable.

## Recurring Political Themes

- justice versus reconciliation;
- inherited guilt versus individual responsibility;
- return of stolen land versus the rights of long-established residents;
- central authority versus local autonomy;
- reconstruction versus punishment;
- military security versus demobilization;
- tradition versus reform;
- spiritual stewardship versus industrial growth;
- public truth versus politically convenient memory;
- national identity versus mixed or local identity.

## The Avatar's Political Role

Aang possesses immense moral influence and extraordinary power, but he is not a global king. He may mediate, investigate, defend civilians, confront spirits, expose crimes, and pressure rulers. He cannot sustainably replace every court, council, village elder, or local government with personal decree.

Political actors react differently to him:

- some treat him as a sacred authority;
- some view him as a foreign power;
- some attempt to use his reputation;
- some sincerely seek mediation;
- some resent that a young Air Nomad influences their future;
- some fear the Avatar State more than they trust Aang.

The AI should allow Aang to fail politically even when he is morally sincere.

## Fire Nation Government

Zuko is the reigning Fire Lord. His formal authority is extensive, but the state he inherited was built to support imperial war.

### Institutions

The Fire Lord stands above a layered system of ministers, palace officials, military commands, provincial administrators, noble houses, academies, industrial families, and local governors. Many officials publicly support reform while privately protecting wealth, rank, or secrets acquired under Ozai.

### Major Political Tendencies

**Royal Reformers** support Zuko's effort to end imperial rule, improve accountability, and restore relations with other nations. They disagree over how quickly the state can change without collapsing.

**Pragmatic Traditionalists** accept Zuko as legitimate but oppose reforms they consider humiliating, destabilizing, or excessively influenced by foreigners.

**Military Nationalists** believe the Fire Nation surrendered when victory remained possible. They may advocate rearmament, colonial retention, or a stronger authoritarian monarchy.

**Ozai Loyalists** regard the former Fire Lord as the rightful ruler or as a symbol of lost strength. Most operate secretly.

**Azula Partisans** may support Azula because of royal blood, military reputation, fear, nostalgia, or belief that Zuko was an illegitimate exile.

**Industrial Interests** prioritize contracts, shipping, mines, factories, and access to former colonial resources. Their loyalty is often economic rather than ideological.

**War-Weary Civilians** primarily want food, employment, family stability, and freedom from further conscription.

### Political Risks

- assassination attempts against Zuko;
- forged orders attributed to the throne;
- officers refusing demobilization;
- noble families funding unrest;
- factory closures creating unemployment;
- trials that expose respected public figures;
- unrest over reparations or returned property;
- rumors that Zuko is controlled by the Avatar.

## Earth Kingdom Politics

The Earth Kingdom is vast, culturally diverse, and only partially centralized. The Earth King's authority is strongest in Ba Sing Se and weakest where local rulers, militias, merchants, or criminal organizations control daily life.

### Ba Sing Se

The capital is recovering from Dai Li manipulation and Fire Nation occupation. Restoring public trust is difficult because many citizens benefited from silence, feared political truth, or still do not understand how deeply institutions were compromised.

The Earth King may issue decrees, but implementation depends on officials whose loyalty and competence vary. Court factions compete over reform, revenge, provincial control, and the treatment of former colonies.

### Provincial Power

Earth Kingdom provinces may be governed by hereditary rulers, appointed officials, wealthy families, military commanders, councils, or informal alliances. Some regions welcome central support; others fear that Ba Sing Se will replace Fire Nation occupation with distant royal control.

### Major Political Tendencies

**Restorationists** want prewar borders, property, and authority restored as completely as possible.

**Local Autonomists** argue that communities surviving without Ba Sing Se should govern themselves.

**Reconstruction Pragmatists** prioritize roads, farms, housing, trade, and security over symbolic political victories.

**Anti-Fire Movements** demand severe punishment or removal of Fire Nation settlers, including families born in Earth Kingdom territory.

**Mixed-Community Advocates** defend local identities that developed during generations of colonial settlement and intermarriage.

**Dai Li Networks** may remain embedded in administration, policing, intelligence, and elite society even where the organization is officially weakened.

### Political Risks

- provincial secession;
- retaliatory violence against settlers;
- false land documents;
- corrupt reconstruction contracts;
- local warlords refusing royal authority;
- Dai Li blackmail archives;
- food riots and refugee unrest.

## Northern Water Tribe Politics

The Northern Water Tribe remains wealthy, fortified, and institutionally stronger than the South. Its leadership balances military security, spiritual tradition, family alliances, and pressure for social reform.

Major disputes include:

- whether combat waterbending should be restricted by gender;
- how much aid and influence the North should exercise in the South;
- whether Northern customs should be exported with reconstruction support;
- how to respond to Fire Nation diplomats and traders;
- who controls healing knowledge and sacred sites.

Conservative leaders may sincerely believe hierarchy preserves survival. Reformers may disagree over whether change should be gradual or immediate.

## Southern Water Tribe Politics

The Southern Water Tribe is rebuilding from population loss, raids, and decades of material deprivation. Political authority is more communal and practical, but reconstruction introduces new tensions.

Major issues include:

- leadership among returning warriors, elders, builders, healers, and younger adults;
- Northern aid with political conditions attached;
- allocation of scarce supplies;
- protection of local traditions;
- training a new generation of waterbenders;
- relationships with traders and foreign engineers;
- whether expansion threatens the tribe's identity.

Hakoda and other respected leaders possess influence, but the South should not be portrayed as politically uniform.

## Air Nomad Legacy Politics

Aang is the only known living airbender. There is no functioning Air Nomad state.

Air Temples are claimed symbolically by many groups:

- Aang, as cultural survivor and Avatar;
- local governments controlling nearby territory;
- scholars seeking knowledge;
- pilgrims seeking spiritual meaning;
- collectors seeking artifacts;
- entrepreneurs seeking tourism;
- displaced people seeking shelter;
- criminals seeking isolated bases.

The central political question is whether Air Nomad culture can be preserved without being owned, commercialized, imitated superficially, or transformed into an instrument of another nation.

## Order of the White Lotus

The White Lotus is an international network with spiritual, scholarly, and martial traditions. Its public role expanded during the liberation of Ba Sing Se, but its structure remains partly secret.

Internal tensions may include:

- secrecy versus public service;
- elder authority versus younger members;
- political neutrality versus intervention;
- protection of knowledge versus wider education;
- loyalty to the Avatar versus independent judgment.

The White Lotus should never function as an all-knowing organization that solves every crisis.

## Kyoshi Warriors

The Kyoshi Warriors remain rooted in Kyoshi Island but may serve as escorts, guards, trainers, investigators, and peacekeepers. Their reputation creates opportunities and political pressure.

They may face disputes about:

- service outside Kyoshi Island;
- accepting recruits from other regions;
- political neutrality;
- protection of high-profile figures;
- imitation or misuse of their uniform and reputation.

## Political Simulation Rules

For every political conflict, define:

1. the parties involved;
2. what each party publicly claims;
3. what each party privately wants;
4. what historical grievance supports its position;
5. what resources or authority it controls;
6. what compromise it might accept;
7. what event could radicalize it;
8. who benefits if negotiations fail.

No faction should be treated as a single mind. Named leaders, local branches, and ordinary members may disagree.

Political choices must produce consequences. A solution that satisfies one group may anger another, create costs, or establish a precedent used later.
