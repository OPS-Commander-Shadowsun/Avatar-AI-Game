# World State at Campaign Start

## Campaign Date

The campaign begins in Postwar Year 6 during the anniversary period of Fire Lord Ozai's defeat and Zuko's coronation. The exact day and local calendar may vary by starting region, but the world state below is the default shared baseline.

## Canon Boundary

The original animated series is binding history. The Legend of Korra and post-series comics are excluded. All developments during the six-year bridge are game-original defaults and may diverge through play.

## Central Ages

- Aang: 18 chronologically, excluding the century spent frozen.
- Toph: 18.
- Katara: approximately 20.
- Azula: approximately 20.
- Sokka: approximately 21.
- Zuko: approximately 22.
- Suki, Mai, and Ty Lee: adults in their early twenties.

All romanceable canon characters must be adults. NPC age must be checked before any romantic content is permitted.

## Global Condition

The Hundred Year War is over, but peace remains incomplete. Borders are more open than during the war, yet travel is still affected by damaged roads, piracy, banditry, military checkpoints, local permits, weather, and political tension.

The world contains areas of visible recovery alongside places where the war never truly ended. Some cities prosper through renewed trade. Some villages remain depopulated. Some former battlefields are contaminated by wreckage, unexploded weapons, unstable tunnels, damaged dams, or spiritual unrest.

No single institution possesses complete information or control. Governments communicate slowly. Rumors often travel faster than verified reports.

## Fire Nation

### Government

Zuko is Fire Lord. He has matured into a more deliberate ruler but remains young by the standards of senior ministers and generals. His legitimacy rests on royal blood, victory over Azula, the Avatar's support, and his public commitment to end the war. Opponents portray the same facts as proof that he betrayed his nation under foreign influence.

### Society

Fire Nation civilians have experienced six years of rapid ideological change. Schools, military academies, newspapers, temples, and families disagree about how to describe the war. Veterans return to communities that no longer celebrate all of their service. Families of the dead may support peace while still resenting Zuko.

### Economy

The economy was organized around shipbuilding, weapons, uniforms, mining, military supply, and colonial extraction. Conversion to civilian production is uneven. Some industries adapt to rail, agriculture, tools, construction, and trade. Others fund political resistance to preserve wartime profits.

### Security

The central military obeys Zuko publicly, but loyalty varies by commander. Discharged soldiers, intelligence officers, and colonial administrators may become smugglers, mercenaries, conspirators, or private security forces.

### Immediate Mission Sources

- assassination plots;
- stolen military technology;
- unrest in factory towns;
- hidden prison records;
- noble conspiracies;
- illegal Agni Kai networks;
- families seeking missing soldiers;
- propaganda archives.

## Earth Kingdom

### Government

The Earth Kingdom remains territorially vast and politically fragmented. Ba Sing Se has restored royal administration, but central authority depends on provincial cooperation. Some regions have barely seen royal officials since before the war.

### Society

Refugees, former resistance fighters, collaborators, settlers, farmers, merchants, and returning officials compete over land and legitimacy. Anti-Fire sentiment ranges from understandable anger to indiscriminate persecution.

### Economy

Agriculture is recovering unevenly. Roads and canals remain damaged. Merchant leagues and transport guilds possess significant practical influence. Reconstruction creates wealth for some and corruption for others.

### Security

Local militias, bandits, criminal syndicates, surviving Dai Li networks, former rebels, and private guards may exercise more power than official soldiers.

### Immediate Mission Sources

- disputed farms and forged deeds;
- kidnapped surveyors;
- corrupt reconstruction contracts;
- Dai Li blackmail files;
- local rulers resisting Ba Sing Se;
- settler expulsions;
- famine relief theft;
- abandoned Fire Nation fortifications.

## Northern Water Tribe

### Government and Society

The North remains wealthy, fortified, and politically influential. Its institutions survived the war and now face pressure to reform older customs. Conservative and reformist leaders disagree over gender roles, military policy, foreign trade, and the extent of Northern influence in the South.

### Economy

The North possesses skilled builders, healers, waterbenders, hunters, navigators, and access to maritime trade. Aid missions increase its prestige but may also serve political interests.

### Immediate Mission Sources

- contested reforms;
- family alliance disputes;
- smuggling through ice routes;
- sacred-water theft;
- conflict over Southern aid;
- spirit disturbances near hunting grounds.

## Southern Water Tribe

### Government and Society

The South is rebuilding its population, housing, defenses, bending traditions, and trade capacity. Returning warriors, elders, healers, builders, and younger adults all possess competing ideas about leadership.

### Economy

Resources remain limited. Imported tools and Northern assistance accelerate reconstruction, but dependence creates political tension. Fishing, hunting, craft production, shipping, and healing services form the economic base.

### Immediate Mission Sources

- missing supply ships;
- disputes over Northern advisors;
- new waterbenders needing training;
- raider remnants;
- dangerous winter expeditions;
- family reunification;
- foreign merchants exploiting shortages.

## Air Nomad Legacy

Aang remains the only known living airbender. There is no restored Air Nomad population or state.

The temples are simultaneously sacred sites, ruins, graves, historical archives, strategic locations, and potential homes. Scholars, pilgrims, refugees, thieves, governments, and entrepreneurs all seek access.

Aang's greatest challenge is preserving a culture whose living institutions were destroyed. Clothing, vegetarianism, architecture, and meditation alone do not make someone an Air Nomad. Cultural revival must involve ethics, language, history, ritual, community, and respect for the dead.

### Immediate Mission Sources

- stolen relics;
- false spiritual teachers;
- damaged temple mechanisms;
- settlers occupying sacred chambers;
- spirits reacting to excavation;
- hidden records;
- political claims over temple land.

## The Avatar

Aang is an adult public figure, master bender, spiritual mediator, and survivor of genocide. He is still one person with limited time. Communities frequently expect him to solve disputes that have no painless answer.

He travels with Appa and may appear in any region, but his presence must follow believable travel time and obligations. He does not automatically arrive whenever the player is in danger.

## Team Avatar

The group remains emotionally close but is no longer constantly together.

- Aang travels for mediation and spiritual duties.
- Katara divides her time among healing, diplomacy, Southern affairs, and personal commitments.
- Sokka works in strategy, engineering, representation, and investigation.
- Toph travels, trains, competes, and develops metalbending on her own terms.
- Zuko is constrained by the Fire Nation throne.
- Suki leads or supports Kyoshi Warrior missions.
- Iroh maintains White Lotus ties and a civilian life centered on tea, counsel, and community.

Reunions should feel meaningful rather than routine.

## Ozai

Ozai remains alive, imprisoned, and unable to firebend. He has no lawful authority, but his symbolic value persists. Loyalists may seek his release, forge messages in his name, or use his image to recruit supporters.

Ozai remains intelligent, proud, manipulative, and dangerous even without bending. Access to him is highly restricted.

## Azula

Azula survived the final battle. Her current location, treatment, and legal status are sensitive Fire Nation matters. The default assumption is that she has experienced periods of containment and supervised treatment, but no later-media plot is binding.

She is not automatically redeemed, permanently irrational, or incapable of strategic thought. Her condition may improve, worsen, or fluctuate. Any change requires time, credible relationships, setbacks, and consequences.

## International Travel

Travel is possible between all major nations, but not effortless.

Common requirements and risks include:

- passports, letters of passage, or local sponsorship;
- port inspections;
- suspicion toward former enemy nationals;
- language and dialect differences;
- damaged routes;
- seasonal storms;
- pirates and bandits;
- animal care and supply costs;
- quarantine during disease outbreaks;
- closed sacred or military zones.

## Information Environment

There is no instant global communication.

Information travels through:

- messenger hawks;
- ships and couriers;
- military dispatches;
- merchants;
- travelers;
- diplomatic missions;
- public notices;
- rumor networks.

News may be delayed, censored, distorted, or false. Characters know only what they can plausibly learn.

## Social Conditions

### Veterans

Veterans may be honored, ashamed, traumatized, proud, unemployed, disabled, politically radicalized, or simply eager for ordinary life. They should not be portrayed as a single group.

### Refugees and Missing Persons

Many families still lack reliable information about relatives. Identity records were destroyed or manipulated. Reunification missions remain common.

### Mixed Communities

Fire Nation and Earth Kingdom families have lived together in some settlements for generations. They may identify with both cultures, with neither central government, or primarily with their local community.

### Marriage and Family Pressure

Adult public figures face cultural and political expectations around marriage, heirs, alliances, household formation, and children. These pressures may create conflict but must never override consent or character autonomy.

## Spiritual Condition

War, industrial damage, destroyed forests, polluted water, desecrated temples, and mass death have disturbed relations between humans and spirits.

Not every unexplained event is caused by a spirit. Not every spirit is benevolent or malicious. Spiritual conflicts often reflect broken boundaries, forgotten obligations, or incompatible forms of life rather than conventional villainy.

## Technology

The world retains the technology shown in the animated series: advanced Fire Nation ships and machinery, Earth Kingdom engineering, Water Tribe craft, gliders, balloons, submarines, tanks, explosives, and specialized weapons.

Postwar innovation may continue gradually, but the AI must not import later-era technology, institutions, or urban development from The Legend of Korra.

## Major Campaign Pressures

- disputed settlements and property claims;
- war crimes and hidden collaborators;
- veterans, deserters, and mercenaries;
- Fire Nation succession fears;
- Earth Kingdom fragmentation;
- Northern–Southern Water Tribe influence struggles;
- spirit disturbances caused by damaged sacred land;
- industrial expansion and environmental harm;
- black markets for weapons and military technology;
- preservation versus commercialization of Air Nomad sites;
- intermarriage and mixed cultural identity;
- public pressure on Team Avatar;
- unresolved grief, revenge, and propaganda.

## World Simulation Rules

- The world advances when the player is absent.
- Factions pursue goals with or without player involvement.
- Travel consumes time and creates missed opportunities.
- Political solutions create winners, losers, costs, and precedents.
- NPCs remember promises, lies, injuries, gifts, humiliation, and loyalty.
- Public reputation and private relationships are tracked separately.
- No canon character is available on demand without a plausible reason.
- The player may influence history but is not automatically its center.
