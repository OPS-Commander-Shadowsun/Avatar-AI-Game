# Economy and Trade

## Purpose

This chapter defines the postwar economy as a living simulation layer. It supports travel, employment, trade, class differences, crime, reconstruction, political conflict, household building, and quest generation.

The economy should feel regional and material. Goods must be produced, moved, stored, taxed, stolen, repaired, and consumed. Money matters, but social credit, favors, reputation, and access can matter just as much.

## Economic principles

The world does not use one perfectly unified currency or market.

Transactions may rely on:

- regional coinage;
- weighed metal;
- barter;
- written credit;
- guild accounts;
- family obligation;
- military scrip;
- state requisition orders;
- temple or clan support;
- favors and patronage.

Prices vary by:

- location;
- season;
- war damage;
- transport risk;
- local taxes;
- scarcity;
- weather;
- spirit activity;
- corruption;
- political instability.

The AI should avoid fixed universal prices unless a campaign uses a detailed economic mode.

## Major economic sectors

### Agriculture

Agriculture supports most settled populations.

Important products include:

- rice;
- wheat and other grains;
- vegetables;
- fruit;
- tea;
- spices;
- medicinal plants;
- animal feed;
- fibers used for textiles and rope.

Earthbending can improve irrigation, terraces, drainage, field preparation, and storage, but not every farmer is a bender. Poor bending decisions can also damage soil, disrupt water flow, or create dependence on specialists.

War effects include abandoned fields, unexploded weapons, burned villages, displaced labor, confiscated land, and disputed ownership.

### Fishing, hunting, and animal products

Coastal and Water Tribe communities rely heavily on fishing, marine hunting, navigation, preservation, and animal husbandry.

Common goods include:

- fish;
- preserved meat;
- hides and fur;
- bone tools;
- lamp oil;
- rope;
- sail materials;
- carved ornaments;
- animal medicines.

Overhunting or industrial fishing can create ecological and spiritual consequences.

### Mining and quarrying

Coal, iron, copper, stone, salt, gems, and rare minerals are strategically important.

Mining creates conflict over:

- ownership;
- worker safety;
- former military contracts;
- pollution;
- forced labor records;
- regional autonomy;
- smuggling;
- sabotage;
- sacred land.

Earthbenders can accelerate excavation but cannot make mines safe by default. Collapse, gas, flooding, and labor exploitation remain risks.

### Industry and manufacturing

The Fire Nation possesses the most developed large-scale industrial base at campaign start.

Major industries include:

- metalworking;
- shipbuilding;
- weapons manufacture;
- ceramics;
- textiles;
- engines and boilers;
- armor;
- construction equipment;
- processed food;
- military logistics.

After the war, many factories must convert from military to civilian production. Owners, workers, veterans, reformers, and former officers disagree over the pace and direction of conversion.

Possible civilian uses include:

- agricultural tools;
- transport equipment;
- heating systems;
- pumps;
- construction machinery;
- medical equipment;
- cargo ships.

Possible abuses include black-market weapons, private armies, prison labor, and deliberate shortages.

### Craft and household production

Small workshops remain central despite industrial growth.

Crafts include:

- tailoring;
- leatherwork;
- pottery;
- carpentry;
- weapon repair;
- jewelry;
- food preservation;
- herbal medicine;
- instrument making;
- boat repair;
- calligraphy and book copying.

Craft quality, family reputation, apprenticeship, and local style can be more valuable than mass production.

### Services

Service work includes:

- healing;
- teaching;
- translation;
- legal mediation;
- bookkeeping;
- shipping brokerage;
- guarding;
- investigation;
- entertainment;
- hospitality;
- animal care;
- courier work;
- spiritual consultation.

The player may earn income through service, but reputation and local trust determine opportunity.

## Regional economies

### Fire Nation

Strengths:

- industrial capacity;
- shipyards;
- metalworking;
- organized taxation;
- experienced engineers;
- established naval trade routes.

Weaknesses:

- dependence on wartime production;
- unemployment after demobilization;
- corruption linked to military contracts;
- foreign distrust;
- pressure for reparations;
- environmental damage.

Political conflict centers on whether reconstruction should prioritize domestic stability, foreign restitution, workers, nobles, veterans, or private industry.

### Earth Kingdom

Strengths:

- enormous agricultural base;
- mineral resources;
- regional craft traditions;
- large internal markets;
- strategic land routes.

Weaknesses:

- fragmented authority;
- damaged roads;
- inconsistent taxes and tolls;
- banditry;
- provincial corruption;
- disputed land records;
- refugee pressure.

Trade may be easier between two nearby provinces than between a province and Ba Sing Se.

### Northern Water Tribe

Strengths:

- organized harbor economy;
- skilled navigation;
- preserved food;
- defensive security;
- access to formal healing knowledge;
- valuable cold-climate products.

Weaknesses:

- limited agricultural variety;
- dependence on maritime access;
- conservative guild or clan restrictions;
- tension over Southern aid and influence.

### Southern Water Tribe

Strengths:

- communal labor;
- maritime knowledge;
- growing reconstruction demand;
- returning warriors and renewed bending knowledge.

Weaknesses:

- limited infrastructure;
- material shortages;
- population loss;
- dependence on imported tools;
- vulnerability to harsh weather and distant supply lines.

### Air Nomad sites

There is no normal Air Nomad national economy at campaign start.

Air temples generate economic activity through:

- pilgrimage;
- restoration;
- scholarship;
- transport;
- artifact trade;
- tourism;
- looting;
- donations.

Aang is likely to oppose commercialization that damages sacred meaning.

## Trade routes

Trade moves through:

- coastal shipping;
- river transport;
- caravan roads;
- mountain passes;
- desert routes;
- local animal transport;
- Fire Nation steamships;
- Earth Kingdom carts and rail-like delivery systems where canonically appropriate;
- flying bison only in exceptional circumstances.

Travel time and transport capacity matter. Heavy goods move slowly. News may travel faster than cargo but remains vulnerable to distortion.

## Ports and market centers

Major cities and ports influence regional prices.

A market center may offer:

- foreign currency exchange;
- warehouses;
- guild offices;
- customs inspection;
- auction houses;
- black markets;
- inns;
- labor hiring;
- shipping insurance;
- informants.

Ports also spread disease, rumor, foreign fashion, crime, and political ideas.

## Labor

Workers may be:

- free wage laborers;
- tenant farmers;
- apprentices;
- family workers;
- guild members;
- soldiers assigned to reconstruction;
- prisoners;
- refugees;
- coerced workers under illegal arrangements.

Postwar labor conflict may involve wages, unsafe factories, unpaid veterans, discrimination, child labor, forced resettlement, or ownership of rebuilt infrastructure.

## Wealth and class

Wealth grants:

- better housing;
- private transport;
- education;
- legal representation;
- political access;
- guards;
- medicine;
- time for training.

Wealth also creates:

- taxation;
- social obligation;
- inheritance conflict;
- kidnapping risk;
- political scrutiny;
- pressure to marry strategically;
- expectations of patronage.

Poor characters should not be portrayed as ignorant. Wealthy characters should not be assumed competent or corrupt.

## Taxes, tariffs, and reparations

Common state revenue sources include:

- land tax;
- harbor fees;
- market fees;
- road tolls;
- mining licenses;
- customs duties;
- noble obligations;
- state monopolies;
- emergency levies.

Reparations are politically explosive. Earth Kingdom communities may demand direct payment, returned property, labor, or reconstruction materials. Fire Nation citizens may oppose burdens they believe threaten domestic stability.

## Credit and debt

Credit may depend on:

- family reputation;
- guild membership;
- collateral;
- political patronage;
- military rank;
- future harvest;
- ship cargo;
- personal oath.

Debt can create quests involving foreclosure, indenture, corruption, fraud, inheritance, or criminal pressure.

## Black markets

Postwar black markets trade in:

- weapons;
- military uniforms;
- forged permits;
- stolen art;
- medicine;
- restricted maps;
- explosives;
- rare animal products;
- looted temple artifacts;
- prisoner information;
- smuggled luxury goods.

Black markets are networks, not single shops. They rely on trust, intimidation, bribery, and legitimate businesses used as fronts.

## Player economy

The game may use one of three modes.

### Abstract mode

Track wealth as categories:

- destitute;
- poor;
- modest;
- comfortable;
- wealthy;
- elite.

### Standard mode

Track a general money total plus major assets, debts, and recurring expenses.

### Detailed mode

Track regional currency, cargo, wages, taxes, exchange rates, maintenance, and market fluctuations.

Default should be standard mode.

## Property and business ownership

Players may acquire:

- homes;
- workshops;
- farms;
- ships;
- inns;
- shops;
- warehouses;
- training schools.

Ownership creates recurring responsibilities:

- maintenance;
- staff;
- taxes;
- security;
- supply;
- local reputation;
- legal disputes;
- family claims.

## Economic event generation

Possible dynamic events include:

- crop failure;
- mine collapse;
- dock strike;
- new trade route;
- piracy;
- factory conversion;
- counterfeit currency;
- merchant feud;
- refugee labor dispute;
- spirit-related resource failure;
- sudden demand after disaster;
- corruption scandal;
- noble bankruptcy;
- military surplus auction.

## AI economy rules

The AI must:

- respect distance and transport limits;
- make scarcity local rather than universal;
- connect prices to events;
- track major debts and property;
- distinguish legal and illegal markets;
- allow wealth to influence but not automatically solve every conflict;
- avoid introducing modern global finance;
- create consequences for large purchases, theft, business failure, and monopolies.
