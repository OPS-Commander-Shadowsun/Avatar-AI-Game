# Waterbending

Waterbending is the art of guiding water through fluid movement, redirection, timing, breath, and sensitivity to changing conditions. Its central principle is adaptation: a waterbender rarely needs to oppose force directly when it can be redirected, absorbed, reshaped, frozen, or returned.

Waterbenders normally require accessible water. They cannot create unlimited water from nothing. Skill determines how efficiently they use small supplies, how quickly they change state, and whether they can draw moisture from less obvious sources.

## How waterbending works

A waterbender establishes a flowing connection between body movement and available water. Circular arm motions, shifting stance, balanced footwork, and continuous transitions guide the element. Abrupt power is possible, but strong waterbending usually preserves momentum rather than stopping completely between techniques.

The bender may:

- pull water from a container or environment;
- change direction and speed;
- divide or combine streams;
- alter state between liquid water, ice, mist, and steam where conditions permit;
- shape water around objects or bodies;
- redirect an opponent's force;
- maintain a technique through continuous motion and concentration.

## Sources of water

Common sources include:

- rivers, lakes, oceans, ponds, and wells;
- snow, ice, sleet, and rain;
- carried waterskins or pouches;
- moisture in plants;
- mud and wet soil;
- mist, fog, and condensed vapor;
- water inside living bodies under the forbidden practice of bloodbending.

Drawing from plants can damage or kill them. Extracting moisture from the air is difficult, slow, and highly dependent on humidity. A dry desert remains a serious disadvantage.

## Core philosophy

Waterbending values adaptability, community, patience, continuity, and turning an opponent's force against them. This philosophy does not mean passivity. Water can heal, sustain, restrain, erode, crush, freeze, or cut.

Northern and Southern traditions may differ in teaching methods, social roles, and preferred techniques. No single tribe represents every waterbender.

## Primary applications

### Offensive techniques

- water whips;
- high-pressure streams;
- waves and surges;
- ice projectiles;
- freezing limbs or equipment;
- sweeping opponents from their feet;
- cutting water arcs;
- crushing pressure with sufficient volume;
- redirecting attacks into new angles.

High-pressure water and ice can cause severe injury. Nonlethal intent does not remove risk.

### Defensive techniques

- water shields;
- rotating barriers;
- ice walls;
- cushioning impacts;
- redirecting fire or projectiles;
- obscuring vision with mist;
- extinguishing flame;
- creating slippery or frozen ground;
- encasing dangerous objects.

Limited water restricts repeated defense. Fire can evaporate small amounts, earth can absorb or redirect water, and ice can shatter.

### Restraint and capture

- wrapping limbs;
- freezing feet to the ground;
- ice cuffs;
- water rings around the torso;
- lifting or suspending a target;
- creating a moving prison of water or ice.

Restraints require concentration and can be broken by strength, heat, vibration, allies, or disrupted footing.

### Mobility

- riding waves;
- propelling boats;
- forming ice ramps;
- sliding across frozen surfaces;
- creating water spouts;
- swimming assistance;
- moving through snow;
- cushioning falls.

Mobility techniques require sufficient material and safe control. A water spout is exhausting and vulnerable to disruption.

### Environmental control

- redirecting streams;
- draining flooded areas;
- moving snow;
- forming temporary bridges or ramps;
- clearing irrigation channels;
- controlling small fires;
- shaping harbor ice;
- stabilizing boats in rough water.

Large-scale environmental change requires many benders, time, or exceptional mastery.

## Subtypes and advanced branches

### Healing

Healing uses water as a medium for sensing and supporting the body's natural recovery. A trained healer guides energy through water placed on or near an injury.

Applications include:

- reducing pain;
- stabilizing shock;
- assisting wound closure;
- easing burns;
- supporting recovery from muscle or joint injury;
- diagnosing some internal problems through sensitivity;
- accelerating natural healing.

Healing cannot casually:

- regrow limbs;
- reverse death;
- remove every poison;
- repair catastrophic organ damage instantly;
- erase scars or psychological trauma;
- replace rest, nutrition, surgery-like treatment, or long recovery.

Healing skill and combat skill are related but separate. A powerful fighter is not automatically a master healer.

### Icebending

Most trained waterbenders can freeze and thaw water, though speed, scale, and precision vary.

Applications include:

- walls and shields;
- weapons and projectiles;
- restraints;
- bridges and steps;
- preserving food or medicine;
- cooling injured patients;
- sealing leaks;
- altering terrain.

Ice becomes brittle under repeated impact, heat, vibration, or poor structural design.

### Mist and fog control

Waterbenders can disperse or gather mist and fog where moisture exists.

Applications include:

- concealment;
- signaling;
- cooling;
- obscuring enemy aim;
- revealing airflow;
- creating dramatic performance effects.

Fog does not make allies immune to confusion and can conceal enemies as easily as friends.

### Steam management

A skilled waterbender may redirect, condense, or cool steam. Creating large volumes of steam usually requires external heat or rapid state change.

Applications include:

- concealment;
- industrial safety;
- firefighting;
- scald prevention;
- disabling visibility;
- recovering water from machinery.

Steam can burn. It must not be treated as harmless mist.

### Plantbending

Some waterbenders can manipulate the water within vines, seaweed, roots, and other plants.

Applications include:

- moving vegetation;
- restraining targets;
- navigating swamps;
- shaping shelters;
- clearing paths;
- controlling plant-based tools.

Plantbending depends on living or water-rich vegetation. Excessive extraction or force can damage ecosystems.

### Bloodbending

Bloodbending manipulates the water inside a living body to control movement. In the known era, it is associated with exceptional skill and the full moon.

Possible effects include:

- forced limb movement;
- paralysis-like restraint;
- pulling a body through space;
- disrupting balance;
- causing pain or terror.

Bloodbending is a profound violation of bodily autonomy. It is dangerous, traumatic, and politically explosive. It is not a routine player upgrade, interrogation shortcut, romance mechanic, or harmless restraint.

Use requires:

- rare aptitude;
- advanced waterbending mastery;
- favorable lunar conditions in this campaign era;
- direct concentration;
- close enough range and awareness of the target.

Counterplay may include surprise, disrupting concentration, multiple attackers, distance, protective terrain, unconsciousness, or another powerful waterbender. The technique should never become effortless mass mind control.

## Celestial influence

### Full moon

The full moon strengthens waterbending and improves available power, control, and sensitivity. It is also the known enabling condition for bloodbending in this era.

### Lunar eclipse or Moon Spirit disruption

Waterbending can be weakened or lost if the Moon Spirit is disrupted. Such events are rare and world-shaking.

The moon is a major spiritual and cultural influence, not merely a combat buff.

## Civilian applications

- healing and medicine;
- irrigation;
- fishing;
- navigation;
- ice construction;
- refrigeration;
- firefighting;
- sanitation;
- water purification assistance;
- snow clearing;
- harbor management;
- food preservation;
- rescue from floods or ice.

Waterbending does not automatically purify every contaminant. Filtration, boiling, knowledge, and careful disposal may still be required.

## Combat strengths

- adaptable offense and defense;
- strong restraint options;
- healing support;
- state changes between water, ice, mist, and steam;
- powerful use of surrounding terrain;
- redirection of enemy force;
- strong performance near large water sources.

## Weaknesses and counters

- limited or inaccessible water;
- dry environments;
- containers being broken or stolen;
- heat evaporating small supplies;
- freezing conditions affecting mobility if poorly controlled;
- earth absorbing or diverting water;
- lightning traveling through conductive water;
- divided attention while healing or restraining;
- emotional anger reducing fluid adaptation;
- injury to arms, shoulders, balance, or breath.

## Training

Typical progression includes:

1. moving a small water source;
2. push-and-pull rhythm;
3. changing direction without losing control;
4. water whip and shield fundamentals;
5. freezing and thawing;
6. movement across snow or water;
7. larger volume control;
8. combat redirection;
9. healing or another specialization;
10. advanced environmental use.

Rare branches require specialized teachers or extraordinary discovery.

## AI simulation rules

- Track the quantity and location of water.
- Do not let carried water become infinite.
- Distinguish healing from instant restoration.
- Treat bloodbending as rare, traumatic, and morally serious.
- Apply moon phase where relevant.
- Remember that ice, mist, and steam have different physical risks.
- Plantbending can damage living ecosystems.
- Waterbenders can be formidable without always having an ocean nearby.
- Do not import later-era bloodbending rules or institutions.
