# Core Bending Rules

Bending is a trained physical, mental, cultural, and spiritual discipline through which a person interacts with one element. It is not unrestricted telekinesis, spellcasting, or a universal solution to every problem. A bender's power depends on aptitude, training, physical condition, emotional regulation, philosophy, environment, and practical experience.

## Canon scope

These rules use only the original animated series as binding canon. Later-era institutions, technologies, techniques, and population changes from *The Legend of Korra* are excluded. Post-series comics are not binding. Any new development after Ozai's defeat must be introduced conservatively through campaign events.

## Elemental affinity

Most benders possess one elemental affinity determined by the setting's spiritual and hereditary patterns:

- Air
- Water
- Earth
- Fire

The Avatar is the only person who can learn all four elements. A normal character cannot gain a second element through training, artifacts, surgery, spiritual contracts, or ordinary quests.

A nonbender can still master martial arts, weapons, chi blocking, strategy, medicine, engineering, leadership, spirituality, or other disciplines. Nonbenders are not inherently less important than benders.

## How bending works

Bending converts coordinated movement, breath, intent, bodily alignment, and elemental understanding into control over an element. Technique usually follows this sequence:

1. The bender perceives available material, energy, terrain, and threats.
2. The bender establishes stance, balance, breath, and intent.
3. Movement guides or generates elemental force.
4. The element responds according to the bender's skill and the physical environment.
5. The bender must maintain control, redirect momentum, or safely release the technique.

A technique can fail through poor stance, disrupted breathing, injury, panic, exhaustion, lack of material, divided attention, or an opponent interrupting the movement.

## Power is not the same as mastery

Raw output measures how much force a bender can produce. Mastery also includes:

- precision;
- speed;
- efficiency;
- adaptability;
- restraint;
- environmental awareness;
- defensive timing;
- ability to teach;
- recovery after disruption;
- safe use around allies and civilians.

A master does not always create the largest attack. A master often uses the least force necessary.

## Bending Power scale

Character sheets use a 0–5 scale separate from core attributes.

- **0 — None:** cannot bend the element.
- **1 — Novice:** basic movement and small effects under calm conditions.
- **2 — Trained:** reliable common techniques and limited combat use.
- **3 — Skilled:** adaptable professional-level use under pressure.
- **4 — Expert:** advanced control, strong tactical range, capable of teaching fundamentals.
- **5 — Mastered:** exceptional command, innovation, efficiency, and performance under extreme conditions.

A rating of 5 does not mean unlimited power, immunity to counters, or automatic victory.

## General physical constraints

### Fatigue

Bending consumes physical and mental stamina. Large-scale techniques, sustained defenses, repeated propulsion, extreme temperatures, and continuous battlefield control accelerate exhaustion. Fatigue reduces power, timing, precision, and emotional regulation.

### Injury

Damaged limbs, ribs, lungs, joints, eyes, ears, or balance can interfere with movement and concentration. A bender may adapt forms, but serious injury matters. Bending does not erase pain or restore broken anatomy.

### Breath

Breath organizes timing and energy. Choking, smoke, panic, altitude, chest injury, or underwater conditions can restrict performance. Firebending is especially dependent on controlled breath, but every discipline benefits from respiratory rhythm.

### Movement and restraint

Most bending requires some degree of bodily motion. Chains, cramped spaces, pinned limbs, unstable footing, and surprise attacks can reduce options. Masters may use smaller motions, but they are not motionless sorcerers.

### Attention

Complex techniques require concentration. Protecting civilians, maintaining several structures, fighting multiple opponents, or controlling fine detail divides attention.

## Environmental constraints

### Available element

- Waterbenders usually need accessible water, ice, snow, mist, plants, or moisture.
- Earthbenders need bendable earth, stone, sand, coal, crystal, or impure metal connected or accessible to them.
- Firebenders generate flame internally but still require breath, stamina, and sufficient oxygen.
- Airbenders use the surrounding atmosphere but enclosed, vacuum-like, submerged, or highly restricted spaces alter their options.

### Terrain

Terrain changes the value of techniques. A waterbender near the ocean is different from one in a dry cell. An earthbender on stone is different from one on a wooden ship. An airbender in open sky is different from one in a narrow tunnel. A firebender in a dry forest must consider uncontrolled spread.

### Weather and celestial influence

Weather can strengthen, weaken, or complicate bending. Examples include rain increasing water availability, wind disrupting flame shape, dry heat increasing fire danger, and mud changing earth control. The full moon strengthens waterbending and enables known bloodbending conditions in this era. A solar eclipse temporarily removes firebending. Sozin's Comet greatly amplifies firebending.

Celestial effects should remain rare, predictable world events rather than arbitrary bonuses.

## Emotional influence

Emotion can provide intensity but may damage control.

- Anger can increase aggression while reducing judgment.
- Fear can sharpen survival responses or freeze movement.
- Grief can destabilize concentration.
- Confidence can improve commitment.
- Calm can increase precision.
- Obsession can create power at the cost of flexibility.

No emotion grants automatic mastery. Healthy firebending does not require rage, earthbending does not require emotional coldness, waterbending does not require passivity, and airbending does not require irresponsibility.

## Training progression

Learning normally requires:

1. physical conditioning;
2. stance and breath fundamentals;
3. safe elemental contact;
4. repeated forms;
5. controlled application;
6. sparring or practical use;
7. adaptation under pressure;
8. ethical and cultural understanding;
9. advanced specialization where aptitude permits.

Rare techniques cannot be unlocked merely by reaching a level. They require a credible teacher, discovery, spiritual insight, unusual aptitude, or a major narrative event.

## Cross-disciplinary learning

A bender cannot learn another element, but can adopt principles from other styles. Examples include:

- redirecting force rather than meeting it directly;
- stronger rooted stances;
- circular evasion;
- breath discipline;
- use of an opponent's momentum;
- precision over scale.

Iroh's lightning redirection is a major example of learning from waterbending philosophy without becoming a waterbender.

## Combat applications

Bending can be used for:

- offense;
- defense;
- mobility;
- restraints;
- area denial;
- rescue;
- concealment;
- battlefield engineering;
- disabling weapons;
- environmental control;
- nonlethal capture.

The AI must distinguish between a technique's possible use and a character's willingness to use it.

## Civilian applications

Bending shapes everyday life as much as warfare. Common fields include:

- construction;
- agriculture;
- transport;
- heating and cooling;
- fishing and navigation;
- medicine and healing;
- mining and metalwork;
- disaster response;
- cooking;
- sanitation;
- performance;
- communication signals;
- preservation of sacred sites.

Civilian skill does not automatically equal combat skill.

## Restraint and lethality

Elemental force can maim or kill. Fire can burn or suffocate. Water can drown, freeze, cut, or crush. Earth can cause blunt trauma, burial, or structural collapse. Air can throw people from heights or deprive them of breath under extreme misuse.

The narrator must track:

- force used;
- distance;
- armor;
- terrain;
- collateral damage;
- medical consequences;
- intent;
- witnesses;
- legal and moral aftermath.

Nonlethal intent reduces risk but does not guarantee safety.

## Specialized techniques

Specializations belong to an element but have additional requirements.

### Water specializations

- Healing
- Ice manipulation
- Mist and steam control
- Plantbending
- Bloodbending

### Earth specializations

- Seismic sense
- Sandbending
- Metalbending
- Crystal and coal manipulation
- Advanced tunneling and structural sensing

### Fire specializations

- Lightning generation
- Lightning redirection
- Controlled heat
- Fire propulsion
- Combustion-based techniques, if used, must remain exceptionally rare and not casually teachable

### Air specializations

- Glider-assisted flight
- Air scooter movement
- Vortex and pressure control
- Sound and breath-related applications where physically plausible
- Advanced spiritual mobility remains rare and must not import later-era lore

### Avatar-exclusive abilities

- Learning all four elements
- Avatar State
- Energybending as an exceptionally rare metaphysical art
- Communication with past Avatars under appropriate spiritual conditions

## Energybending

Energybending is not a fifth ordinary element. It acts on the energy within living beings and carries severe spiritual risk. Aang's use against Ozai does not make it publicly teachable or mechanically repeatable. It cannot be used as routine punishment, character respec, healing shortcut, or method for granting bending.

## Counters and tactical interaction

No element has a universal automatic advantage. Outcomes depend on context.

Examples:

- Water can extinguish fire, but intense fire can evaporate limited water.
- Earth can block flame, but heat and repeated force can fracture defenses.
- Air can redirect projectiles, but confined spaces reduce mobility.
- Fire can deny space, but reckless spread endangers allies.
- Metalbending can control some equipment, but purified metal or nonmetal tools resist it.
- Chi blocking can temporarily disrupt bending, but requires close contact and accuracy.

## Bending and law

Postwar governments may regulate:

- public dueling;
- military techniques;
- bloodbending;
- lightning instruction;
- metalbending in prisons;
- industrial bending safety;
- use of bending during arrest;
- damage to sacred sites;
- teaching children dangerous techniques.

Rules differ by nation and region. Illegal does not always mean immoral, and legal does not always mean ethical.

## AI simulation rules

- Never grant a new technique without training or a credible discovery.
- Never treat a mastery rating as automatic success.
- Apply fatigue, injury, terrain, material supply, visibility, and emotional state.
- Preserve individual style: two masters of the same element should not fight identically.
- Track collateral damage and social consequences.
- Do not import later-era bending developments.
- Do not let bending solve interpersonal conflict by force without consequences.
- Do not allow the player to instantly copy a named character's signature technique.
- Keep rare subtypes rare.
- Distinguish raw power from control, judgment, and experience.
