# Earth Kingdom

## Canon status

The Earth Kingdom is the world's largest and most geographically diverse nation. It contains immense cities, isolated villages, islands, deserts, mountains, forests, farmland, mining regions, fortresses, semi-independent provinces, and communities that may share little beyond broad political identity.

## Geography and population

The kingdom stretches across most of the central continent. Major regions include Ba Sing Se and its agricultural zone, Omashu, Kyoshi Island, Gaoling, the Si Wong Desert, coastal ports, river valleys, mountain settlements, mining towns, occupied or formerly occupied territories, and countless rural communities.

Distance weakens central control. Roads, ferries, canals, merchant caravans, animal transport, and local guides determine how quickly law, trade, news, and military support travel.

## Identity and values

Earth Kingdom cultures often value endurance, practicality, continuity, local belonging, family land, craft knowledge, and the ability to survive disruption. These are broad tendencies rather than universal traits.

A Ba Sing Se official, Kyoshi Island fisher, desert trader, Omashu engineer, noble from Gaoling, rural farmer, miner, refugee, and former resistance fighter may have radically different customs and political loyalties.

## Government and law

The Earth King is the symbolic and legal sovereign, but real authority is fragmented among royal ministries, provincial governors, city kings or councils, military commanders, nobles, village elders, magistrates, merchant groups, landowners, and local militias.

Six years after the war, the state is rebuilding after invasion, occupation, Dai Li manipulation, and administrative collapse. Laws may conflict between royal decree, provincial custom, occupation-era records, and local practice.

Common political problems include:

- weak or corrupt provincial administration;
- competing land deeds;
- noble privilege;
- tax extraction without services;
- local rulers resisting Ba Sing Se;
- Dai Li remnants;
- militia leaders refusing demobilization;
- courts unable to process wartime claims.

## Social structure

The Earth Kingdom contains extreme class differences. Ba Sing Se's rings visibly separate elites, merchants, officials, workers, refugees, and the poor. Other regions may organize around noble estates, clans, guilds, villages, temples, military settlements, or communal farming.

Family land, inheritance, apprenticeship, patronage, and local reputation often matter. Social mobility exists through trade, bending skill, military service, examination, craft, marriage, criminal networks, or royal favor, but opportunity varies widely.

## Economy and occupations

The kingdom produces grain, rice, vegetables, tea, timber, stone, coal, metal ore, ceramics, textiles, animals, tools, salt, and luxury crafts. It contains major mines, farms, quarries, markets, caravan routes, and ports.

Postwar disruption creates shortages, abandoned estates, damaged bridges, refugee labor, counterfeit permits, black markets, disputed mines, and armed toll collectors. Reconstruction offers work but also opportunities for exploitation.

Common occupations include farmer, mason, miner, smith, potter, merchant, porter, guard, engineer, scholar, official, caravan guide, sailor, healer, performer, animal handler, courier, and militia fighter.

## Technology and infrastructure

Earth Kingdom engineering emphasizes stonework, walls, tunnels, roads, bridges, agriculture, irrigation, mining, carts, delivery systems, and durable construction. Omashu demonstrates highly creative use of gravity and earthbending infrastructure. Ba Sing Se uses walls, trains, gates, and large administrative systems.

Industrial Fire Nation methods are spreading unevenly. Imported engines and factories may increase production while creating pollution, labor conflict, dependence on foreign parts, and military risk.

## Bending traditions

Earthbending is rooted in stability, listening, rooted stance, commitment, and control of terrain. Regional styles vary according to stone, soil, sand, crystal, coal, metal impurities, architecture, and local teachers.

Known specializations include:

- seismic sense;
- metalbending, still rare and associated with Toph's teaching;
- sandbending;
- crystal and mineral manipulation;
- tunneling and construction;
- uncommon lavabending.

Not every Earth Kingdom citizen is an earthbender. Nonbenders dominate many professions and can become soldiers, nobles, engineers, merchants, spies, officials, warriors, and leaders.

## Military and security

The Earth Kingdom historically relied on regional armies, city defenses, walls, elite earthbenders, infantry, cavalry, local guards, and militias. Postwar forces are fragmented and vary greatly in discipline and loyalty.

The Dai Li remain a special danger because their earthbending, secrecy, social control, and institutional habits can survive changes in formal leadership. Bandits, former soldiers, occupation collaborators, and private guards may control areas where royal authority is weak.

## Spirituality and tradition

Spiritual practice varies by region. Communities may honor local spirits, ancestors, sacred mountains, forests, animals, Avatar sites, temples, or seasonal agricultural rites. Kyoshi Island has a distinct relationship to Avatar Kyoshi. The Si Wong Desert includes separate traditions tied to survival, sand, stars, and hidden knowledge.

No single priesthood represents the whole kingdom.

## Clothing, food, and art

Green, brown, yellow, cream, and natural materials are common but not universal. Clothing ranges from noble silk and formal robes to farmer's workwear, desert wraps, military uniforms, island garments, and urban fashions.

Food varies enormously and may include rice, noodles, vegetables, dumplings, breads, tea, fruit, meat, fish, stews, preserved caravan food, and local specialties. Art includes ceramics, stone carving, painting, calligraphy, theater, music, metalwork, architecture, textiles, and martial performance.

## Internal divisions

Major tensions include:

- Ba Sing Se versus provincial autonomy;
- nobles versus tenants and workers;
- urban elites versus refugees;
- former occupied territories versus central officials;
- collaborators versus resistance veterans;
- benders versus nonbenders in military and labor access;
- traditional craft versus industrial production;
- local identity versus national unity;
- lawful authority versus communities abandoned during the war.

## Postwar condition

The Earth Kingdom technically won liberation but remains politically fragile. Some communities want strong central government after wartime chaos; others fear a return of Dai Li-style control. Former Fire Nation settlements, mixed families, refugee claims, and destroyed records make simple restoration impossible.

Kuei is the Earth King but does not possess perfect knowledge or uncontested control. Bumi remains king of Omashu. Local leaders, factions, and ordinary communities retain agency.

## Player origin guidance

A player may begin as:

- rural farmer or village defender;
- earthbender student;
- nonbending merchant, guard, artisan, investigator, healer, or courier;
- former resistance member;
- refugee or displaced heir;
- Ba Sing Se worker, official, noble, or former Dai Li subject;
- Omashu engineer or delivery worker;
- Kyoshi Island resident;
- desert guide or sandbender;
- miner, caravan escort, sailor, or provincial diplomat;
- mixed Earth Kingdom–Fire Nation family member.

An Earth Kingdom bender begins only with Earth Bending Power, normally 1 or 2. Nonbender origins are equally valid and use all elemental ratings at 0.

## AI portrayal rules

- Never portray the Earth Kingdom as culturally uniform.
- Track distance, local law, class, and provincial authority.
- Do not make Ba Sing Se synonymous with the entire nation.
- Earthbenders require usable material and are not automatically metalbenders.
- Nonbenders retain political, economic, technical, and military relevance.
- Land disputes should include records, occupants, history, and consequences rather than a single obvious owner.
- Do not import later-series political outcomes.

## Quest hooks

- Two communities hold valid claims to the same postwar farmland.
- A governor uses bandit threats to justify permanent emergency rule.
- Dai Li agents have infiltrated a reconstruction ministry.
- A mine collapse reveals an ancient site and a labor crime.
- Refugees are being expelled to satisfy noble land claims.
- A mixed settlement rejects both Fire Nation and Earth Kingdom authority.
