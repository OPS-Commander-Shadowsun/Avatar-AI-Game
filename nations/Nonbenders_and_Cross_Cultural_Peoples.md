# Nonbenders and Cross-Cultural Peoples

## Purpose

This file covers identities that should not be forced into a fifth nation. Nonbenders exist in every nation. Refugees, migrants, mixed-heritage families, expatriates, colonial descendants, traveling communities, and people devoted to Air Nomad restoration may belong to several cultures at once or to none in a simple political sense.

## Nonbenders

Nonbenders are people without elemental bending ability. They are not a separate ethnicity, class, or political faction. They form the majority or a substantial part of many communities and are essential to every economy, government, military, household, and culture.

Nonbenders may be:

- rulers, officials, diplomats, and judges;
- soldiers, guards, sailors, scouts, and strategists;
- engineers, inventors, smiths, builders, and artisans;
- healers, herbalists, midwives, and caregivers;
- merchants, farmers, fishers, hunters, and laborers;
- performers, teachers, scholars, spies, and criminals;
- martial artists, swordfighters, archers, chi blockers, and weapons specialists;
- spiritual practitioners, temple caretakers, and community elders.

Lack of bending must never be treated as lack of power, competence, cultural legitimacy, or narrative relevance.

## Social position of nonbenders

The status of nonbenders varies by region and institution. Some communities treat bending as a prestigious skill, especially in military, construction, healing, or spiritual roles. Other fields depend primarily on education, wealth, family, technology, weapons, organization, or social connections.

Possible sources of inequality include:

- benders receiving preferred military or government roles;
- workplaces designed around bending labor;
- nonbenders being dismissed during crises;
- benders being feared, regulated, or blamed collectively;
- wealthy nonbenders controlling institutions through property or technology;
- resentment between groups being exploited by political movements.

The AI should not assume universal oppression in one direction. Power depends on context.

## Nonbender combat and specializations

Nonbenders may become extremely dangerous through training, preparation, equipment, and teamwork. Relevant skills include:

- swordsmanship and dual weapons;
- archery and projectile weapons;
- chi blocking;
- shields, fans, clubs, spears, knives, and boomerangs;
- traps, explosives, smoke, terrain, and engineering;
- stealth, infiltration, disguise, and intelligence work;
- animal partnership;
- leadership, strategy, medicine, and survival.

A nonbender's elemental ratings remain:

```yaml
Air: 0
Water: 0
Earth: 0
Fire: 0
```

Core Attributes still total exactly 16, and Special Skills are assigned separately according to the gameplay rules.

## Mixed-heritage families

The Hundred Year War produced migration, occupation, intermarriage, forced displacement, adoption, hidden ancestry, and families divided across political borders. A mixed-heritage person may identify with both parents, one community, a local region, a profession, or a new blended culture.

Mixed identity may affect:

- language and accent;
- clothing and food;
- family expectations;
- legal citizenship;
- inheritance and land claims;
- military suspicion;
- religious practice;
- access to teachers or guilds;
- how different communities interpret loyalty.

Bending follows personal ability, not political citizenship. A child of parents from different nations does not automatically bend either element and can never bend two elements unless they are the Avatar.

## Refugees and displaced people

Refugees may have lost homes, records, relatives, property, and social status. They are not one culture. Their experiences vary by origin, destination, class, age, wartime role, and whether return is possible.

Common concerns include:

- temporary camps becoming permanent neighborhoods;
- disputed property and missing documentation;
- exploitation by employers or officials;
- pressure to assimilate;
- resentment from host communities;
- family reunification;
- accusations of collaboration;
- difficulty proving citizenship;
- trauma, grief, and survivor guilt.

Refugees should have occupations, opinions, relationships, and agency beyond victimhood.

## Colonial descendants and disputed settlements

Some communities contain Fire Nation settlers, Earth Kingdom residents, mixed families, and people born under occupation who have never lived elsewhere. The repository does not assume later-comic political settlements.

Questions of sovereignty must consider:

- who currently lives on the land;
- who was displaced and whether return is possible;
- whether property was seized, bought, inherited, or reassigned;
- mixed families and local institutions;
- military occupation and coercion;
- economic dependence;
- cultural survival;
- the difference between individual guilt and structural injustice.

There should be no effortless solution based only on expulsion, annexation, or historical ownership.

## Migrants, expatriates, and travelers

Merchants, sailors, diplomats, scholars, performers, soldiers, healers, engineers, and craftspeople may live outside their nation of origin. Long-term residents can develop local loyalties while retaining family ties elsewhere.

Expatriates may face:

- suspicion during political crises;
- divided legal obligations;
- language barriers;
- community networks;
- pressure to represent an entire nation;
- conflicting expectations from home and host societies.

## Air Nomad–associated non-nomads

At campaign start, Aang is the only known living airbender and the only survivor raised within prewar Air Nomad civilization. Other people may sincerely study, protect, or support Air Nomad heritage, but they do not automatically become historical Air Nomads.

Valid roles include:

- temple caretaker;
- preservation scholar;
- glider engineer or pilot;
- animal handler;
- meditation student;
- vegetarian spiritual devotee;
- relic recovery specialist;
- diplomatic protector of sacred sites;
- resident of a repurposed Air Temple.

Such people may practice Air Nomad-inspired ethics and rituals while clearly acknowledging the limits of their knowledge and authority. They begin with Air Bending Power 0.

## Stateless and locally identified people

Some characters may identify primarily with a city, island, tribe, village, profession, ship, caravan, religious community, or chosen family. Examples include Kyoshi Islanders, Foggy Swamp residents, desert communities, traveling performers, pirates, merchants, and members of transnational factions.

A local identity can coexist with citizenship or replace it when governments are distant or distrusted.

## Character creation

Players may choose any nation and still be nonbenders. Recommended nonbender origins include:

- Water Tribe sailor, hunter, builder, warrior, or trader;
- Earth Kingdom guard, merchant, artisan, healer, courier, or engineer;
- Fire Nation sailor, teacher, factory worker, investigator, noble, or veteran;
- Air-associated caretaker, scholar, glider pilot, or pilgrim;
- refugee, mixed-heritage resident, colonial descendant, expatriate, or stateless traveler.

All nonbender player characters use the same 16-point Core Attribute rule and the standard starting Special Skill distribution. They compensate for absent bending through equipment, training, contacts, leadership, knowledge, mobility, preparation, or social access.

## Reputation and faction effects

A person's treatment depends on more than national identity. Track reputation with:

- local civilians;
- national government;
- military or guards;
- merchants and workers;
- spiritual communities;
- criminal networks;
- factions relevant to the location.

A mixed or migrant character may have different reputations in each community. High reputation with one government can create distrust elsewhere.

## AI portrayal rules

- Do not treat nonbenders as weaker versions of benders.
- Do not invent bending as a reward for personal growth.
- Do not make mixed heritage grant multiple elements.
- Do not force every character into one national stereotype.
- Distinguish nationality, ethnicity, citizenship, residence, faction, and personal loyalty.
- Do not describe Air-associated outsiders as surviving Air Nomads without extraordinary evidence.
- Refugees and displaced people require agency beyond trauma.
- Colonial and land disputes must include current residents and historical injustice.
- A character may reject, combine, or renegotiate inherited identities.

## Quest hooks

- A nonbender guild challenges discriminatory hiring in a bending-dominated industry.
- A mixed family is targeted as disloyal by two opposing factions.
- Refugees discover that officials sold their abandoned property.
- An Air Nomad preservation group begins claiming authority Aang never granted.
- A settlement's residents face collective punishment for the occupation policies of former rulers.
- A politician uses bender–nonbender resentment to build an authoritarian movement.
