# Air Nomads

## Canon status

The Air Nomads were one of the world's four historic peoples. Their civilization was destroyed at the beginning of the Hundred Year War. At campaign start, Aang is the only known living airbender and the only surviving person raised inside prewar Air Nomad society. The culture may be studied, honored, and partially restored, but it cannot be casually recreated from fragments or later-era material.

## Geography and historical population

Air Nomad life centered on four remote temples built into mountains, cliffs, and isolated highlands. Their settlements were connected through sky-bison travel rather than roads or territorial borders. The temples included sanctuaries, residential chambers, kitchens, training courts, meditation spaces, gardens, animal roosts, libraries, workshops, and ceremonial halls.

Historically, Air Nomads were highly mobile. Temples were cultural anchors rather than capitals of territorial states. Their population included airbenders, monks, nuns, children in communal education, caretakers, artisans, cooks, healers, animal handlers, and travelers. Not every aspect of their society is fully documented at campaign start.

## Identity and philosophy

Air Nomad culture emphasizes freedom, compassion, detachment from possessiveness, humor, self-knowledge, mobility, and reverence for life. Detachment does not mean emotional indifference. It means recognizing that fear, ownership, status, and ego can distort judgment.

Important cultural values include:

- nonviolence and avoidance of unnecessary harm;
- vegetarianism and respect for animals;
- spiritual discipline through meditation and reflection;
- curiosity, travel, play, and creative problem solving;
- communal responsibility rather than dynastic inheritance;
- limited attachment to wealth and permanent property;
- humility about power.

These values must not be reduced to passivity. Air Nomads could be disciplined, stubborn, humorous, scholarly, adventurous, and formidable when defending life.

## Governance and authority

The Air Nomads did not function like a hereditary monarchy or centralized territorial state. Senior monks and nuns guided temples, education, spiritual practice, and community decisions. Authority was based on experience, trust, teaching responsibility, and cultural role rather than noble birth.

At campaign start no legitimate Air Nomad government exists. Aang carries extraordinary moral and cultural authority but is not automatically entitled to invent any custom he wants. Decisions about ruins, relics, students, devotees, and restoration should involve preservation ethics, informed consent, local communities, and acknowledgement that Aang is both survivor and imperfect interpreter.

## Family and childhood

Children were raised communally within temple life rather than primarily through small hereditary households. Teachers, peers, elders, and animal companions formed strong family-like bonds. Biological ancestry was less politically important than education and community.

A restoration movement must not assume that communal upbringing means absence of love or identity. It also must not pressure Aang or any future children to reproduce an extinct culture solely for demographic reasons.

## Education and training

Traditional education combined:

- airbending forms;
- meditation and spiritual study;
- ethics and nonviolent conflict resolution;
- history, stories, music, games, and crafts;
- glider use and maintenance;
- animal partnership;
- travel and observation of other cultures;
- practical communal work.

Mastery tattoos represent genuine airbending mastery and cultural recognition. They are not fashion. Non-airbenders must not receive authentic mastery tattoos merely for admiration or service.

## Bending tradition

Airbending is integrated with breath, awareness, circular movement, evasion, redirection, and freedom of motion. It is not merely a combat technique. Traditional airbending education is inseparable from philosophy, play, travel, and spiritual discipline.

At campaign start:

- Aang is the only known airbender;
- no ordinary player origin may begin as an airbender;
- claims of hidden airbender communities require campaign-defining evidence;
- gliders and Air Nomad practices can be learned by nonbenders, but they do not grant bending;
- spiritual devotion does not create airbending.

## Nonbender roles and Air-associated outsiders

Nonbenders may become preservationists, historians, temple caretakers, glider engineers, animal handlers, cooks, translators, guards, pilgrims, or students of Air Nomad ethics. Such people are Air-associated, not automatically Air Nomads.

Possible terms include temple caretaker, Air Nomad devotee, preservation pilgrim, or student of Air philosophy. The AI should describe the person's specific relationship rather than assigning a false ethnic identity.

## Economy and property

Historical temple communities relied on gardens, stored food, craft production, exchange, hospitality, and travel networks. They did not organize life around large private estates or commercial accumulation.

In the postwar era, temple ruins create disputes involving:

- ownership of relics;
- tourism and pilgrimage;
- excavation rights;
- looted artifacts;
- local guides and merchants;
- conservation labor;
- donor influence;
- military access to strategic heights.

Aang may reject commercial exploitation while still needing supplies, workers, and international support.

## Spiritual life

Air Nomad spiritual practice is central rather than decorative. Meditation, sacred spaces, breath discipline, seasonal observances, statues, personal teachers, and relationships with animals and spirits all matter.

Not every Air Nomad was equally enlightened, and spiritual language must not be used to excuse avoidance, secrecy, or control. Sacred locations may contain grief, damaged spiritual balance, or spirits angered by desecration.

## Clothing, food, art, and daily life

Common visual traditions include yellow, orange, red, layered robes, practical travel clothing, shaved heads among many practitioners, and blue mastery tattoos for airbending masters. Clothing varies by temple, role, weather, age, and task.

Food is vegetarian and may include fruit, vegetables, grains, breads, dumplings, soups, teas, nuts, and preserved travel provisions. Art may include music, carving, murals, calligraphy, statues, toys, glider decoration, and stories combining moral teaching with humor.

A peaceful daily rhythm may involve meditation, shared work, lessons, bending practice, animal care, meals, study, play, maintenance, and open time.

## Postwar condition

The Air Nomads remain a destroyed civilization, not a functioning nation. Their temples are damaged, looted, occupied, repurposed, or difficult to reach. Aang faces conflicting demands:

- preserve without freezing the culture into a museum;
- teach without inventing false certainty;
- accept sincere help without allowing appropriation;
- protect sacred places without creating an authoritarian religious state;
- build a future while grieving the past.

The Northern Air Temple includes residents and technology developed during the war. Their home and survival cannot be erased simply to restore ruins to an imagined original condition.

## Player origin guidance

A player may be:

- a nonbending temple caretaker;
- a glider pilot or engineer inspired by Air Nomad designs;
- a historian or relic-recovery specialist;
- an animal handler assisting Appa or temple wildlife;
- a spiritual pilgrim studying under Aang;
- a former looter seeking restitution;
- a diplomat assigned to protect temple neutrality.

Such characters begin with Air Bending Power 0. They may learn cultural knowledge, meditation, glider operation, spirituality, animal handling, preservation, or diplomacy.

## AI portrayal rules

- Do not treat Air Nomad identity as a costume or personality aesthetic.
- Do not create casual new airbenders.
- Do not make Aang omniscient about every lost custom.
- Distinguish restoration, study, devotion, and ethnic-cultural continuity.
- Preserve the genocide's emotional and political consequences.
- Keep Air philosophy capable of humor, discipline, disagreement, and practical work.
- Do not use later-series Air Nation institutions.

## Quest hooks

- A government claims strategic control over an Air Temple.
- Relics sold abroad may have been stolen from burial or memorial spaces.
- Temple residents oppose a restoration plan that would displace them.
- A sincere devotee begins granting false mastery titles.
- A spirit reacts violently to tourism at a sacred site.
- Evidence suggests a surviving sky-bison route, but rival groups seek control of it.
